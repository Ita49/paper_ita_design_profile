01 — Context
Background
By 2011, Zenith Bank PLC had grown into one of Nigeria's top-tier commercial banks — strong balance sheet, expanding branch network, and a growing digital offering. But growth had exposed a fault line: the customer experience was inconsistent across channels. A customer opening an account in-branch had a radically different journey than one attempting it online. There was no shared understanding of how customers actually moved through the bank.
I joined as a Business Analyst embedded in Product Operations. My remit was broad: understand where processes were breaking down and recommend improvements. What I discovered was that the real problem wasn't individual broken steps — it was that no one had ever mapped the full picture.
Constraints
Three separate channels (Branch, Online, Call Centre) operated independently — data was not shared between them
No existing journey documentation — this work would be created from scratch through direct observation and interviews
Regulatory context (CBN guidelines) shaped what could be redesigned versus what was fixed process
Problem Statement
"The bank had three channels and three completely different notions of who the customer was and where they were in their journey."

02 — Discovery & Research
Three channels.
No shared truth.
Over eight weeks I conducted customer intercept interviews at three branch locations, shadowed contact centre agents across two shifts, and audited complaint logs going back 18 months. The patterns that emerged weren't subtle — they were structural.

Insight 01
The branch knows nothing the app knows
Customers switching channels had to repeat their entire history. A customer who applied online and then visited a branch was treated as a new enquiry. Data lived in silos — CRM, branch system, and digital platform were separate ecosystems.
Insight 02
Account opening lost customers mid-journey
Online account opening had six steps and no progress indicator, no save state, and no confirmation after submission. Complaint logs showed 40%+ of initiated applications never completed. No one had flagged this because no one owned the full funnel.
Insight 03
Issue resolution was invisible to everyone
Customers couldn't track their complaints. Agents couldn't see cross-channel history. Managers had no aggregate view. Issues averaging 11 days to close were reopened an average of 1.8 times because root cause wasn't documented.
Insight 04
Front-line staff were absorbing system failures
Branch staff were improvising workarounds daily — WhatsApp groups, personal spreadsheets, informal escalation chains. They were holding the experience together through heroic effort rather than good process. This was invisible to leadership.

Key Decision
Map the current state first — no one wanted to admit how broken it was until they saw it drawn
There was pressure to jump straight to recommendations. I held the line: we ran a current-state journey mapping workshop with branch, digital, and contact centre leads together. When they saw the complete map — the gaps, handoff failures, and channel blind spots — in one room for the first time, the conversation shifted from "is there a problem?" to "where do we start?"

06 — Learnings
What this shaped
in how I work
Making the invisible visible is a design act
The most impactful moment wasn't a design decision — it was putting the current-state map on a screen in a room with people from three separate departments. Visualisation changed the conversation. Senior stakeholders who had resisted change for years agreed to act within the same session. The artefact did the persuasion, not the presentation.
Current-state mapping before future-state design
I've seen this skipped in every organisation I've worked in since. The pull towards solutions is enormous — stakeholders want answers, not questions. But without a rigorous current-state map, future-state designs are built on assumptions. I now treat current-state documentation as non-negotiable, not optional groundwork.
Cross-channel alignment needs a shared artefact, not another meeting
Three teams had talked about cross-channel coordination for years. The journey map was the first thing they'd all looked at together. A well-made artefact holds a conversation that no one else has to facilitate. It became the standing reference — printed, pinned, and cited — long after the project formally closed.
