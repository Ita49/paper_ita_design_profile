01 — Context
Background
Paystack, a Stripe Group company, operates one of Africa's largest payment infrastructure platforms. Behind the merchant-facing product sits a large internal operations team — risk analysts, compliance officers, and fraud investigators — responsible for monitoring transactions, managing suspicious cases, and filing regulatory reports with financial authorities.
When I joined the project, these teams were working across disconnected tools: a legacy internal dashboard, multiple Jira boards, shared spreadsheets, and email threads for escalation. No single surface gave an analyst a full picture of a case.
Constraints
Regulatory requirements meant certain audit trails and data retention rules were non-negotiable
Engineering bandwidth was limited — design had to work within existing API structures
Multiple user roles (analyst, reviewer, compliance lead) with different permissions and workflows
Problem Statement
"Risk analysts had no unified view of case history, transaction flags, or compliance status — leading to missed escalations and slow resolution times."
Average case resolution required context from 3–5 separate tools. Escalations were handled via Slack. Regulatory reports were manually assembled in spreadsheets.

02 — Discovery & Research
Method
Stakeholder interviews
8 risk ops analysts and compliance leads across 3 sessions. Focused on daily workflow, pain points, and escalation patterns.
Method
User shadowing
Observed 4 analysts working live cases — documenting tab switches, copy-pasting between tools, and workarounds.
Method
System audit
Mapped all 7 tools in active use. Documented what each one held, gaps, and duplication between systems.
Method
Journey mapping
Mapped the full case lifecycle — from flag trigger through analyst assignment, review, decision, and regulatory filing.
Key Insights
Context switching is the core cost
Analysts averaged 12–15 tab switches per case. The mental load of reassembling context was the biggest source of errors and slowdowns — not the complexity of the cases themselves.
Escalation had no system
High-risk cases were escalated via Slack DMs. There was no audit trail, no SLA visibility, and no way to see which cases had been escalated at a glance.
Compliance reporting was manual
Monthly regulatory reports were assembled by hand — pulling from 3 spreadsheets and the legacy dashboard. One team member owned this entirely, creating a critical single point of failure.
Roles had different needs
Analysts needed deep case detail. Reviewers needed an approvals queue. Compliance leads needed aggregate data and export. One tool had been trying to serve all three with the same interface.

Key Design Decision
Role-based views over a single shared interface
Early designs tried to serve all three roles — analyst, reviewer, and compliance lead — from the same layout. Testing revealed this created confusion: reviewers were overwhelmed by case detail they didn't need, and leads couldn't get to aggregate data quickly.
We restructured around role-based entry points with a shared case detail view — each role lands in a tailored workspace, then drills into shared case data when needed. This reduced cognitive load and cut time-to-decision for reviewers by an estimated 40%.

06 — Learnings
01
Map before you wireframe
The service blueprint sessions with the team — before a single wireframe existed — were the most valuable design work on this project. They surfaced role conflicts, missing states, and regulatory requirements that would have caused expensive redesigns at hi-fi stage. I'd start here on any complex internal tool.
02
Internal users need the same rigour as consumers
There's a bias — in product teams and in myself — to treat internal tooling as a lower tier of design work. The research here proved otherwise. These analysts worked in this tool for 6–8 hours a day. Poor information hierarchy, ambiguous status labels, and missing affordances had a direct cost in accuracy and time.
03
What I'd do differently
I'd push harder for a structured beta with a subset of analysts before full rollout. We launched to the full team simultaneously, which meant edge cases and role-specific needs surfaced all at once. A phased rollout with a feedback loop built in would have made the post-launch iteration phase less reactive.
