01 — Context
Background
Paystack, a Stripe Group company, is one of Africa's largest payment infrastructure platforms serving tens of thousands of businesses. Before any merchant can accept payments, they must pass a formal KYC (Know Your Customer) review — a regulatory requirement from the Central Bank of Nigeria and international compliance standards.
When I joined the onboarding design effort in 2018, merchants submitted documents via email, compliance officers reviewed them across four disconnected tools, and applicants had no visibility into where their case stood.
Constraints
CBN regulatory requirements defined mandatory document types and data retention rules — non-negotiable
Existing API infrastructure had to be reused — no greenfield data model
Multiple user roles: applicant, compliance reviewer, compliance lead, system admin — each with different permissions and workflows
Problem Statement
"Compliance officers had no unified view of an application — documents lived in email, decisions in spreadsheets, and status updates in Slack. Merchants waited up to 7 days with no feedback."

02 — Discovery & Research
How I researched the problem
I ran a two-week discovery sprint with compliance officers, business operations, and a sample of merchants who had recently completed or failed the onboarding process. Research methods included contextual user shadowing, stakeholder interviews, journey mapping, and a full audit of the existing system's touchpoints.

Insight 01
Context switching was the core cost
Officers spent 35–40% of review time navigating between tools. The mental load of reconstructing context — not the review itself — was the main source of errors and slowdowns.
Insight 02
Merchants wanted status, not speed
Interview data showed merchants were less frustrated by wait time than by silence. No status updates generated more support tickets than any other pain point in the process.
Insight 03
Rejections had no structured feedback
Rejection reasons were emailed as free text by individual officers — inconsistent, often vague. Merchants resubmitted the wrong documents 41% of the time after a first rejection.
Insight 04
60% of rejections were preventable
A document-type analysis revealed that over half of rejections were for missing or invalid files — issues detectable at upload time before an officer ever reviewed them.

Key Decision
One review surface over a role-per-app approach
Early stakeholder proposals suggested separate tools for merchants and compliance officers. I pushed back: two isolated surfaces would recreate the fragmentation we were solving. Instead, we built a single platform with role-based views — merchants see status and document feedback; officers see the full review workspace. Shared data model, different surfaces.
04 — Final Design
Unified KYC Review Portal
The final system consolidated document submission, status tracking, and review decisions into a single role-aware platform — with automated pre-checks reducing manual review load before any officer intervention.

06 — Learnings
01
The compliance officer is a core user
Treating the internal reviewer as secondary delayed the first design iteration by two weeks. The officer's workflow has more decision density than any merchant flow. Next time: internal users get their own discovery sprint, not a footnote in stakeholder interviews.
02
Transparency beats speed for merchants
I assumed merchants primarily wanted faster approvals. Research showed they wanted progress visibility above all else — knowing where they stood removed anxiety even when the timeline didn't change. Status design is underrated in compliance products.
03
Involve infrastructure earlier
The automated pre-check system required deeper engineering partnership than anticipated. Bringing the infra lead into the Define phase — not just the Design phase — would have surfaced API constraints earlier and avoided a significant late-stage design revision.
