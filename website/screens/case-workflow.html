01 — Context
Background
By 2014, Wakanow Group had grown into Nigeria's largest online travel agency — but its back-office operations hadn't scaled with it. Flights, hotels, and package tours were being serviced by separate teams using different, undocumented processes. A booking that hit a problem — cancellation, rate dispute, refund — could move through three teams with no structured handoff and no consistent resolution path.
I joined to map the entire operations workflow from first booking to case closure, then redesign it — starting with the service blueprint and ending with a structured internal case management tool.
Constraints
Three separate operational teams (Ticketing, Support, Finance) each with entrenched processes — change required buy-in, not just design
Airline and hotel supplier SLAs imposed external time constraints on case resolution that were non-negotiable
No existing technology platform to build on — workflow tool had to be designed alongside (not after) the process redesign
Problem Statement
"When a booking hit a problem, there was no standard path. Three teams, three processes — customers received conflicting information and cases fell through the gaps between teams."

02 — Discovery & Research
Six swimlanes, zero documentation
I spent three weeks shadowing agents across all three operational teams, mapping what actually happened — not what the org chart suggested. The result was a 6-swimlane service blueprint covering the customer, front-line agent, team lead, operations manager, supplier, and back-end system. It was the first time anyone at Wakanow had seen the full workflow drawn in one place.

Insight 01
No standard escalation path
Two agents handling an identical cancellation scenario could take completely different actions. Outcome depended on who you got, not what the situation required. Inconsistency was structural, not behavioural.
Insight 02
Handoffs were where cases died
Issue log analysis showed 71% of SLA breaches occurred not within a team but between teams. Information lost at the handoff from Ticketing to Support — or Support to Finance — accounted for the majority of delays and duplicated effort.
Insight 03
Managers had no live visibility
Team leads and operations managers could only discover case status by walking over and asking an agent. There was no dashboard, no queue view, and no way to identify overdue cases without manual check-ins — which happened twice a day at best.
Insight 04
Training was the silent cost
New agents took 6+ weeks to reach competency because process knowledge existed only in people's heads — not in documented SOPs. Each new hire required a senior agent as a shadow for weeks, compounding the capacity problem across the team.

Key Decision
SOPs before screens — get sign-off on the process before designing the tool
There was pressure to deliver a tool quickly. I held the line: designing a case management system before the new workflow was agreed would just automate the broken process. We ran three SOP workshops — one per team — to reach consensus on the redesigned escalation flow before a single wireframe was drawn. The tool then enforced a process everyone had already accepted.

A
SLA age indicator
Case age is displayed in the table and changes colour as deadlines approach — neutral at <12h, amber at 12–24h, red past SLA. Managers see urgency at a glance without manual escalation requests.
B
Structured handoff state
"Pending Handoff" is a distinct status — not just a note on an open case. The receiving team must explicitly accept the handoff before the case moves. This eliminated the information loss that caused 71% of SLA breaches.
C
SOP access in context
Agents can open the relevant SOP directly from the case detail view — no separate system. Standard procedures are embedded in the workflow rather than living in an external document that agents rarely consult.
05 — Outcomes
↓ 85%
Case resolution time
From 3–5 days at baseline to under 18 hours at 60 days post-launch. Structured handoff points eliminated the between-team delays that drove 71% of SLA breaches.
↓ 67%
New agent ramp-up time
From 6+ weeks to 2 weeks. SOPs embedded in the case tool — not in a separate document — meant agents could follow the process from day one without shadowing a senior colleague full-time.

06 — Learnings
01
Process design is service design
The blueprint came before the tool — and that was the right order. The most valuable deliverable of this project wasn't the UI. It was the to-be service blueprint that gave three teams a shared view of how work should flow. The tool enforced it, but the blueprint designed it.
02
Standardisation needs sign-off before screens
Running SOP workshops before wireframing felt slow. But getting each team lead to formally sign off on the redesigned workflow meant adoption was pre-sold. When the tool shipped, agents weren't being asked to follow an unfamiliar process — they were using a tool that matched what they'd already agreed to.
03
Training materials are a design deliverable
The ramp-up reduction from 6 weeks to 2 was only possible because we designed the onboarding guide alongside the product — not after. A workflow tool without an embedded training path is a half-finished handoff. I treat documentation as part of the design scope now, not a post-launch task.
