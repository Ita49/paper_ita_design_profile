01 — Context
Background
Wakanow Group is one of Africa's largest online travel companies, processing thousands of flight and hotel bookings daily across Nigeria and the wider West African market. By 2015, the business was growing fast — but payment fraud and chargebacks were scaling with it.
Fraud analysts worked from raw transaction export files, reviewed disputes via email chains, and had no way to correlate patterns across accounts or flag repeat offenders in real time. I was brought in to map the end-to-end payment and fraud operations, then redesign the monitoring and case management workflow.
Constraints
Multiple payment gateways (local and international) with different data schemas — no unified transaction model
Chargeback dispute windows of 45–120 days depending on card network — time-sensitive decisions
Fraud team of 4 — tooling had to maximise individual analyst throughput, not assume headcount growth
Problem Statement
"Fraud analysts had no shared intelligence. Each analyst worked from a private spreadsheet — patterns were invisible, response times were slow, and chargebacks were reviewed after dispute windows had already closed."

02 — Discovery & Research
Mapping the fraud lifecycle end to end
I ran a three-week discovery sprint embedded with the fraud and payments operations team. Rather than starting with screens, I started with a service blueprint — mapping every touchpoint across the transaction, detection, dispute, and resolution lifecycle across four swimlanes: customer, analyst, finance, and payment gateway.

Insight 01
No shared fraud intelligence
Each analyst maintained a personal spreadsheet. A card flagged by one analyst as compromised was invisible to colleagues — repeat offenders slipped through repeatedly across different analysts' queues.
Insight 02
Disputes reviewed after deadlines
Chargeback notifications arrived via email and were manually triaged. Analysis showed 23% of disputes were actioned after the card network's response window had closed — automatic losses.
Insight 03
Blunt rules blocked real bookings
Velocity-based rules were applied globally — a card used twice in 24 hours was blocked regardless of context. High-value legitimate travellers were declined at the same rate as actual fraud attempts.
Insight 04
Two distinct threat types, one response
Card fraud (stolen card details) and account takeover (compromised login) required fundamentally different detection signals and response workflows — but the existing process treated them identically.

Key Decision
Data model before UI — fraud taxonomy first
I resisted pressure to start wireframing early. Until we had agreed on what constituted a "fraud case" — what fields it required, what states it moved through, how card fraud differed from account takeover — any UI would be built on shifting ground. Three weeks of Define work saved what would have been four rounds of design rework when the data model eventually surfaced edge cases.

A
Shared live feed
All four analysts see the same real-time transaction stream. Flags and notes are shared instantly — no more isolated spreadsheets or missed repeat offenders.
B
Trigger labels
Each flagged transaction shows the specific rule that triggered it. Analysts understand the logic, not just the outcome — which builds trust in the system and reduces manual override errors.
C
Four-state status model
Clear / Flagged / Blocked / Under Review — each state has a distinct colour, label, and permitted action set. Designed to WCAG AA contrast standards throughout.

06 — Learnings
01
Build the data model before the UI
Fraud systems have deep domain complexity. Until the taxonomy of threats, case states, and rule logic was agreed with the data engineering team, wireframes were guesswork. The three weeks spent in Define paid back in avoided rework. I apply this discipline to every high-complexity internal tool now.
02
Analysts need to trust the logic, not just the outcome
Early feedback showed analysts overriding flags they didn't understand rather than acting on them. Showing the specific trigger — "BIN mismatch" rather than just "Flagged" — dramatically increased follow-through. Transparency in automated decisions is a design problem, not just an engineering one.
03
Edge cases revealed more than the happy path
International transactions, multi-currency bookings, and expired dispute windows were treated as edge cases during design. In practice, they made up 34% of fraud analyst workload. Designing for the edges first would have shaped the core interface — not the other way around.
